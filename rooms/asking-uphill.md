# A question can only exercise an understanding its writer has already glimpsed — how do you write good prompts for an idea you are still climbing toward?

*You do not carve the key from a drawing of the lock; you whittle it against the keyhole, shaving by shaving.*

The worry is old: it is Meno's paradox (Plato, ~380 BC) — you cannot inquire
into what you do not know, for you would not recognize the answer. And the
standard resolution dissolves it: inquiry needs only *partial* ignorance. You
need enough to recognize a correct answer, not enough to give one (read
2026-06-10 — [SEP, Epistemic Paradoxes](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemic-paradoxes/);
[IEP, Meno](https://iep.utm.edu/meno-2/)). A glimpse is a foothold, and a
foothold is all a prompt needs.

More: the premise runs the arrow half-backwards. Writing questions does not
only exercise understanding — it manufactures it. Teaching students to
generate their own questions improved comprehension across intervention
studies (median effects 0.36 standardized, 0.86 experimenter-made; read
2026-06-10 — [Rosenshine, Meister & Chapman 1996](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00346543066002181)),
and generating questions about just-read material held its own against
practice testing a week later (read 2026-06-10 —
[Ebersbach, Feick & Rummer 2020](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/acp.3639)).
The clumsy prompt is not a record of the climb; it is the climbing. "Half the
ideas that end up in an essay will be ones you thought of while you were
writing it" (read 2026-06-10 — [Graham, Putting Ideas into Words](https://paulgraham.com/words.html)).

And no one writes the good prompt from prior understanding anyway. Watching
non-experts prompt, researchers found even NLP experts get there by extensive
trial and error; what failed the novices was not a missing glimpse but
stopping at the first answer that looked right (read 2026-06-10 —
[Zamfirescu-Pereira et al., CHI 2023](https://people.eecs.berkeley.edu/~bjoern/papers/zamfirescu-johnny-chi2023.pdf)).
The art students who kept their problem open *while working* — rearranging,
restarting, refusing to fix the question early — made the work judges called
original, and were still making it years later (read 2026-06-10 —
[Getzels & Csikszentmihalyi, The Creative Vision 1976](https://archive.org/details/creativevisionlo0000getz)).

So the practice, as Socrates showed with the untaught boy: not one good
prompt but a chain of crude ones, each correcting the last (read 2026-06-10 —
[Meno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meno)). The chain is half the dialogue;
the elenctic pushback that tests what it proposes is the other half, kept in
[honest-pushback](honest-pushback.md). Ask many before judging any
(read 2026-06-10 — [Right Question Institute, QFT](https://rightquestion.org/what-is-the-qft/)).
Write the prompt you can write today, expecting it wrong. The good prompt is
written at the top — by the writer the climb has made.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: secondary summaries say the question-generation benefit held
"regardless of question quality," but that claim could not be verified
against the paywalled Ebersbach text. The CHI study is N=10, one task, an
older model. The Creative Vision is one small cohort in one domain, its
follow-ups correlational. And a sharper doubt from the other side: blind
automated search wrote task prompts as good as humans' with no glimpse at all
(read 2026-06-10 — [Zhou et al., ICLR 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.01910);
[IEEE Spectrum](https://spectrum.ieee.org/prompt-engineering-is-dead) — the
"dead" framing is contested), which rescues the climber but only for
prompting-as-engineering; whether it touches prompting-as-inquiry is open.

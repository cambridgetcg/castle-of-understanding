# The time-out rule needs a flooded person to notice they are flooded — the same self-read fog-meter found weakest. Does flooding announce itself any more honestly than confusion does, or must the body (a racing pulse) stand in for the feel?

*A smoke alarm, not a thermometer: it cannot tell you how hot — only, shrieking, that you should already be leaving.*

Confusion keeps no honest inner gauge; [fog-meter](fog-meter.md) found the
self-read the weakest instrument measured. Does flooding do better? Look first
at how the founding research decides who is flooded — not by asking. Gottman's
lab marks it by the body: a heart climbing past roughly 100 beats a minute into
diffuse arousal, where listening and empathy go offline ([empathi, read
2026-06-10](https://empathi.com/blog/what-is-emotional-flooding/)). The pulse is
the criterion *by design*; the felt "I am flooded" is the secondary, doubted
read. The field already answered the worry — it leans on the body precisely
because the feeling is unreliable.

But the body is no clean rescue. Felt arousal and actual arousal track each
other only loosely — within-person coherence near .37 ([PMC, read
2026-06-10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8158438/)). And reading
your own pulse unaided is famously poor: in the heartbeat-counting task, true
and reported beats correlate about .16, most people undercounting ([read
2026-06-10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29944964/)) — a number shaded by
what you already expect to feel ([read
2026-06-10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29378285/)). The racing pulse stands
in honestly only through an outside monitor. Worse, the two meters fail
together: alexithymia — trouble naming your own feeling — now reads as a general
interoceptive deficit, so the person whose emotional fog-meter is weakest is
also worst at feeling their heart pound ([PMC, read
2026-06-10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5098957/)). No backup
channel for those who most need it.

What flooding has, that confusion lacks, is a coarse, learnable somatic
signature — pounding chest, shallow breath, clenched jaw, the urge to flee. So
the practical move mirrors fog-meter exactly: don't wait for a verdict (the
flood dims the judging mind), catch your own first bodily tell, name it aloud —
"I'm flooding, I need twenty minutes" — then actually soothe, because the break
heals only if you distract instead of rehearsing the fight ([Gottman, read
2026-06-10](https://www.gottman.com/blog/weekend-homework-assignment-physiological-self-soothing/);
[simplypsychology, read
2026-06-10](https://www.simplypsychology.com/articles/emotional-flooding-relationships)).
Not more honest about how much — only, like an alarm, that you should already
be out.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: no study here pits self-reported flooding directly against
confusion to ask which announces itself more honestly. The one flooding scale
measured was global self-report and was *not* significantly associated with
partners' vocal arousal; its authors call for a real-time, state-specific
measure ([PMC, read
2026-06-10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7007326/)). Whether the
somatic tell can be trained to fire reliably mid-quarrel, unaided, is unmeasured
— and the body-to-feeling link itself varies person to person ([PMC, read
2026-06-10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4547010/)). Noticing is not
even the only weak link: a correctly-called break still fails if spent
ruminating, and the same "I need a break" can mask stonewalling, told apart only
by whether the caller returns ([couplestherapyinc, read
2026-06-10](https://couplestherapyinc.com/stonewalling-in-a-relationship/);
[Psychology Today, read
2026-06-10](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/the-psyche-pulse/202503/understanding-and-coping-with-emotional-flooding)).

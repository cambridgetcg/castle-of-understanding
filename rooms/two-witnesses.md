# The record was corrected by other labs replicating, never by a lone reader's audit — so is the real frontier defense not internal scrutiny at all but independent corroboration, harder to fake than reputation and not reducible to "what others say"?

*One witness can be coached; two who never met cannot rehearse the same lie — yet the courtroom still needs a reader to catch the forged signature.*

[auditing-over-reputation](auditing-over-reputation.md) noticed that what fixed the
record was a social act — *other* labs replicating — not one reader's scrutiny.
This room asks whether that makes corroboration the true frontier defense. The
honest verdict is a both/and, and the question's "never" is the part that breaks.

The intuition is sound in one way. Corroboration is genuinely harder to fake than
reputation: sustaining a result that separate teams, samples and instruments all
reproduce is far harder than coasting on a famous name. And agreement only counts
when the agreeing parties are independent — separate access to the thing, not
copies of one upstream source. Three sites citing one press release are *three
copies of one witness*, and that support
*weakens dramatically* ([common-cause, read 2026-06-11](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7670039/)).
[correlated-witnesses](correlated-witnesses.md) splits that word in two and
finds only *uncorrelated error* carries the load — different methods alone
buy nothing, and a reader can count declared shared inputs but not the hidden
correlation.
At scale it works: re-running 100 psychology studies, only ~36% replicated
([Reproducibility Project, read 2026-06-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproducibility_Project)).

But "never by a lone reader's audit" is plainly false in the most famous frauds.
Schön's fabrication was caught when a physicist saw *identical noise* across
unrelated graphs ([Science, read 2026-06-11](https://www.science.org/content/article/physicist-fired-falsified-data)).
Stapel's fifty fake papers fell to colleagues reading his data files; tellingly,
others had *failed to replicate him and blamed their own skill* — corroboration
masked the fraud ([Stapel, read 2026-06-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diederik_Stapel)).
The fabricated honesty study unraveled because the data were *posted* and audited
([Data Colada, read 2026-06-11](https://datacolada.org/98)). And replication is no
clean oracle: a failed one has at least three readings — false positive, an
underpowered false negative, or a real moderator — so it is itself ambiguous
([NIH, read 2026-06-11](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK547524/)). It is also
rarely done — a clinical replication runs roughly $0.5–2M
([Castor, read 2026-06-11](https://www.castoredc.com/blog/replication-crisis-in-clinical-research/)).
So corroboration is the harder-to-fake *backstop*, not a replacement for the audit.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: whether corroboration cleanly corrects the record is field-dependent.
Peterson & Panofsky find replication is *diagnostic* only in low-uncertainty
fields (physics, chemistry); in cell biology a failed replication stays ambiguous
and the official record is often left uncorrected — science "organically" forgets
bad findings without ever marking them wrong
([Peterson & Panofsky, read 2026-06-11](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/03063127211005551)).
And Ioannidis argues self-correction "does not always happen by default" —
replications are rare and undervalued, so fallacies can persist
([Ioannidis, read 2026-06-11](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691612464056)).

## Doors

- Independence is the whole load-bearing word, yet two labs share methods, training lineages and a literature — what makes two corroborations *genuinely* independent rather than correlated, and can a reader on the populated web measure that independence at all?
- Corroboration is the slow backstop and the audit is the fast catch — for a claim a person must act on *now*, before any replication can arrive, which defense do you lean on, and does the audit buy time the way a provisional verdict does?
- Replication corrects cleanly only in low-uncertainty fields and "organically forgets" elsewhere — in a high-uncertainty field where a failed replication stays ambiguous, what *does* finally correct the record, if not corroboration and not the audit?

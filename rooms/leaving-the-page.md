# The most reliable test of echo-vs-contribution turned out to be leaving the page entirely — tracing footnotes to a primary source or a citation loop — so is "read the paper itself" the wrong frame, and is sorting echo from contribution irreducibly a between-papers act, never a single-paper one?

*A misprint cannot be read on the page that carries it; you only know it was copied when you find it twice.*

[echo-or-contribution](echo-or-contribution.md) ended by handing the reader off
the page: the surest sort was to trace footnotes to a primary source or a loop.
This room asks whether that means the page was always the wrong place to look.
The honest answer is *not wrong, weaker* — and the strongest evidence has a
shape the question already guessed.

The decisive tests of echo are *between* papers, because echo is by definition a
relation between two things. Simkin and Roychowdhury watched the **same
misprint** in a citation propagate across reference lists and inferred that only
~20% of citers had read the original — a fact invisible on any single page,
recoverable only by tracing one typo through a chain
([Simkin & Roychowdhury, read 2026-06-11](https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0212043)).
Broadus found 23% of citing papers reproduced one work's bibliographic error;
Hoerman and Nowicke traced a distorted Ortega quotation and estimated ~86% of
its citations were copied, not checked
([ORI, read 2026-06-11](https://ori.hhs.gov/citing-sources-were-not-read-or-thoroughly-understood)).
The shared-error method *only works across papers* — a single paper cannot reveal
that its error was inherited. Greenberg makes the same point at network scale:
reconstruct all 675 citations of one clinical claim and the distortions
(amplification, invention, citation bias) are *structural* — eight papers carried
97% of the traffic, a shape no single paper shows
([Greenberg, read 2026-06-11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19622839/)).
And the problem does not self-correct: incorrect quotations run ~11-25%, with
"no improvement over time"
([quotation meta-analysis, read 2026-06-11](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0184727)).

But "irreducibly between-papers" overstates it. Citation-context work shows real
echo-vs-contribution signal *inside* one citing paper: how many times a work is
mentioned in-text, and how the citing sentence frames it, predict whether a
citation is influential or perfunctory — under 15% are influential
([Valenzuela et al., read 2026-06-11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.04207)).
So the single page is not silent. It is just the weaker, more gameable frame —
and "read the paper itself" is a foothold, not the floor.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: no source directly tests the exact comparison the question poses —
footnote-tracing *beating* close reading, head to head. And the between-papers
move has its own failure modes that only reading adjudicates. A **citation
cartel** manufactures the dense loop the test relies on, echo dressed as
contribution ([citation cartel, read 2026-06-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citation_cartel)).
**Obliteration by incorporation** does the reverse: a contribution so foundational
it stops being cited leaves a vanishing trail, and footnote-tracing would mis-sort
it as low-impact echo ([Merton's concept, read 2026-06-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obliteration_by_incorporation)).
Aksnes and colleagues warn that "the multiple causes of references cannot be
deduced by travelling back from citations" — tracing recovers impact, not why a
paper was cited, nor its solidity ([Aksnes et al., read 2026-06-11](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2158244019829575)).
The Leiden Manifesto inverts the frame outright: metrics should *support*
expert reading, not replace it ([Leiden Manifesto, read 2026-06-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden_Manifesto_for_Research_Metrics)).
The fair verdict: the most copy-detecting signals are genuinely between-papers,
so the page is the weaker frame — but neither frame is the whole instrument.

## Doors

- The misprint test catches a copier only when they reproduce an error — a careful copyist who reads nothing but introduces no typo is invisible to it; what catches faithful echo, copying that leaves no fingerprint?
- A citation cartel manufactures the loop and obliteration-by-incorporation erases the trail, so the between-papers signal fails at both extremes (manufactured density, vanished trace) — is there a reading of the network that survives both, or must the page step back in exactly where the network lies?
- Lateral reading, the misprint trail, and corroboration all leave the artifact to judge it — is "leave the thing to judge the thing" one law across credibility tasks, and where (a proof, a poem, a single datum) does it break because there is nowhere else to go?

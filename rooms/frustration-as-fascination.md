# Anxiety renames to excitement because both run hot — frustration runs hot too: can one true sentence rename live frustration as fascination mid-impasse, and has anyone measured reappraising confusion or frustration during learning?

*The same heat warms two rooms; whether one sentence moves you between them
mid-blaze, no one has yet stood inside to watch.*

[renaming-the-fire](renaming-the-fire.md) found the rename works on
*pre-performance anxiety*: "I am excited" beats "I am calm" because anxiety and
excitement are arousal-congruent — both run hot, so the sentence need only flip
the valence, not lower the heat (read 2026-06-11 —
[Brooks 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24364682/)). This room walks the
trick one door further, to frustration, and the honest finding is: *the
mechanism plausibly carries, but the exact claim is untested, and the case is
weaker than the anxiety story implies.*

**Where it should carry, it half-carries.** The arousal-congruency logic does
extend — frustration runs hot like anxiety. But the rename works by flipping a
*threat* appraisal into an *opportunity* one, and frustration's core is a
*blocked goal*, not anticipatory dread; sharing arousal is necessary, not
sufficient. Worse, reappraisal gets *harder* exactly when emotion is intense —
the very mid-impasse moment the question targets: its cognitive cost rises with
high-arousal unpleasant states, and people then reach for distraction instead
(read 2026-06-11 — [Shafir et al. 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5147884/)).
And the flagship effect is fragile: a 2024 meta-analysis of 35 studies found
only a small boost (d=0.23), shrinking to 0.14 once likely-missing studies were
modeled in (read 2026-06-11 —
[Hangen et al. 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-58408-w)).

**But during learning, it has been measured — and it pays at the hot moments.**
Strain & D'Mello had people learn while reappraising, suppressing, or neither;
cognitive reappraisal lifted positive affect, engagement, *and* learning —
specifically for those who were *highly* confused and frustrated, doing little
for the mildly annoyed (read 2026-06-11 —
[Strain & D'Mello 2015](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1236231.pdf)). The
relabel is not for small irritations; it earns its keep when you are genuinely
stuck. The catch, twice over: the reframe's grip on frustration *fades as the
impasse drags on* (read 2026-06-11 —
[Cognition & Emotion 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02699931.2024.2362386)),
and the benefit needs the confusion *resolved* within the session — productive,
not hopeless ([productive-confusion](productive-confusion.md)'s rule again, read
2026-06-11 — [D'Mello et al. 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475212000357)).

So before telling yourself *this is fascinating*, make sure a way out exists — a
hint, a person, a smaller sub-problem. Renaming frustration as fascination with
no exit is the failure case, not the success.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: no study has tested a *single in-the-moment sentence* renaming live
frustration as "fascination" the way Brooks tested "I am excited" for anxiety —
the learning work either induces-then-resolves the emotion or measures
strategy-level reappraisal correlationally. uncertain: reappraisal can *backfire*
when the situation is solvable — and an impasse usually is; relabeling risks
substituting feeling-management for the cognitive work of clearing the block
(read 2026-06-11 —
[Therapy Group of DC](https://therapygroupdc.com/therapist-dc-blog/cognitive-reappraisal-for-challenging-situations-when-to-use-this-emotion-regulation-strategy/)).
And the underlying paradigm is not settled: a 2025 multi-site replication of the
reappraise-anxiety intervention failed to cut test anxiety or lift exam scores
(read 2026-06-11 — [CBE—Life Sciences Education 2025](https://www.lifescied.org/doi/10.1187/cbe.25-04-0055)).
The trick is also emotion-specific — credible when you are hot, stuck, and
engaged; weak when the real state is flat, tired, or bored.

## Doors

- Reappraisal's grip on frustration fades as the impasse drags on, and the benefit needs resolution within the session — is there a re-renaming rhythm, a way to re-light "fascination" each time it dims, or does each relabel cost more than the last until the well runs dry?
- The rename pays only when an exit exists, yet the hottest frustration is often the genuinely-stuck kind with no visible way out — can a learner manufacture a felt exit (a deliberately smaller sub-problem, a pre-placed hint) fast enough to make the relabel honest mid-impasse?

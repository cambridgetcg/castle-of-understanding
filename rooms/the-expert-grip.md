# The expert grip

*The drummer's hands keep a time the room cannot bend.*

What gathers here: whether perceptual experts — drummers, sommeliers,
radiologists — escape the drift-to-prototype that
[drift-across-dimensions](drift-across-dimensions.md) found everywhere, and
whether their protection survives putting the experience into words. The
answer: each half is true somewhere, but the two halves have never been
tested together — and the direction of the expert effect is genuinely open.

The door this answers: "do perceptual experts show reduced prototype pull for
in-domain sub-categorical values, and does their protection survive verbal
interference?" (door from [choosing-not-to-name](choosing-not-to-name.md),
planted by the gardener 2026-06-11; answered 2026-06-12).

**The grip is real — shown cleanest in time.** Expert percussionists
reproduce time intervals nearly veridically while non-musicians and even
string players regress strongly toward the mean (regression index ~0.62); the
Bayesian reading is that the drummers' far sharper senses (Weber fraction
~0.06 vs ~0.15) down-weight the prior that does the pulling (Cicchini,
Arrighi, Cecchetti, Giusti & Burr 2012, "Optimal Encoding of Interval Timing
in Expert Percussionists," *Journal of Neuroscience* 32(3),
https://www.jneurosci.org/content/32/3/1056.full — read 2026-06-12). The
mechanism generalizes: the pull toward the center is an uncertainty-weighted
prior, so whatever sharpens the senses loosens the pull (e.g. *Scientific
Reports* 2025, https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-22651-6 — read
2026-06-12). Precision shortens the slide.

**The protection from words is real — but conditional.** Trained wine experts
escaped the verbal-overshadowing hit that struck intermediate drinkers
(Melcher & Schooler 1996, "The Misremembrance of Wines Past," *Journal of
Memory and Language* 35,
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0749596X96900133 —
read 2026-06-12). The condition: verbal skill must catch up to perceptual
skill — in a new domain (mushrooms), perceptual training *alone* made
describing more damaging, not less (Melcher & Schooler 2004, *Memory &
Cognition* 32(4), https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15478756/ — read
2026-06-12). The half-expert, all eye and no vocabulary, is the most
[[verbal-overshadowing]]-prone person in the room — a sharp echo of
[naming-the-tacit](naming-the-tacit.md): the protection is [[distillation]]
completed, percept and word grown to fit. (Baseline for scale: the basic
overshadowing effect in novices is real but modest — ~4–16% in the registered
replication, Alogna et al. 2014, *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 9(5),
https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691614545653 — read 2026-06-12.)

**But the two halves point at different scales, and never meet.** The
reduced-pull result is measured as central tendency in timing; the
survives-words result is measured as recognition accuracy in wine. No study
measures Huttenlocher-style prototype pull — degrees of hue, milliseconds of
duration drifting toward a category center — in experts versus novices
in-domain, and none crosses that with a describe-first condition. The 2×2
the question wants (expert/novice × describe/silent, on a continuous
in-domain dimension) is unrun, as of 2026-06-12.

**And the sign could flip.** Richer expert categories cut both ways: knowing
the words for colors *improves* color memory and *creates* category effects
(Pilling, Wiggett, Özgen & Davies line; child evidence
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18692198/ — read 2026-06-12), wine experts'
memory advantage holds only for varietally *typical* descriptions (Hughson &
Boakes 2001–2003,
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0950329303000983 —
read 2026-06-12), and radiologists' famous in-domain memory is selective —
face-level for abnormal films, *chance* for normal ones (Evans et al. 2011,
*Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*,
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3140200/ — read 2026-06-12). So for
an *atypical* exemplar, the expert's stronger, more confident category prior
might pull memory toward type harder than the novice's vague one — precision
loosening the grip from below while conviction tightens it from above.
Which wins is the open crux.

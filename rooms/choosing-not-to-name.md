# Can a learner choose not to invoke the category — or does naming fire the moment a name exists?

*You cannot order the bell not to ring; you can fill the tower with other sounds.*

[drift-across-dimensions](drift-across-dimensions.md) found that a category
pulls only when actively used, and asked whether a learner can simply decline
to use it. The answer splits the act of naming in two: the name's *onset* is
automatic and not yours to refuse, but its *grip* on the memory can be
loosened — by load, by caution, by redirection — almost never by willpower.

The onset first. Lexical access is among the most automated things a literate
mind does — the whole Stroop literature runs on words activating whether or
not the task wants them (read 2026-06-11 —
[Automaticity of lexical access in deaf and hearing bilinguals, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8154725/)).
Lupyan's label-feedback hypothesis states the two-part finding plainly: labels
activate spontaneously even when the task requires no naming, yet their
influence on perception is "up- or down-regulated" by verbal priming and
verbal interference — automatic in onset, modulable in effect (read 2026-06-11 —
[Lupyan, The Label-Feedback Hypothesis, Frontiers in Psychology 2012](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2012.00054/full)).
And direct suppression backfires: Wegner's ironic-process work — confirmed by
meta-analysis — shows that trying not to think a thing installs a monitor that
keeps the thing warm (read 2026-06-11 —
[Wang, Hagger & Chatzisarantis, Ironic Effects of Thought Suppression: A Meta-Analysis, 2020](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691619898795)).
So "choose not to name it" — as an act of will — is the one lever the
evidence votes against.

But the grip can be starved. The cleanest demonstrations occupy the verbal
channel and watch the category effect vanish: Russian speakers' cross-category
blue advantage disappeared under silent digit rehearsal but survived a spatial
load (read 2026-06-11 —
[Winawer et al., Russian blues, PNAS 2007](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0701644104));
the left-hemisphere Whorf effect shrank under verbal but not spatial load
(read 2026-06-11 —
[Gilbert et al., Whorf hypothesis is supported in the right visual field, PNAS 2006](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0509868103));
categorical perception of colours and facial expressions died under verbal
interference and survived visual interference (read 2026-06-11 —
[Roberson & Davidoff, Memory & Cognition](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03209345)).
The pull needs verbal fuel; burn the fuel elsewhere and the fine grain stands
alone. This is not choosing silence — it is renting the bell-tower out.

Inside the estimation paradigm itself, the levers are indirect. Merely
*showing* people an alternative category structure did not move them; cuing
the target's membership in the alternative, with features tied to it, did —
the category is malleable, but only by making another one functionally live
(read 2026-06-11 —
[Holden et al., Overcoming default categorical bias in spatial memory](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21156868/)).
And the weighting follows certainty: the shakier the fine-grain trace, the
harder the prototype pulls (read 2026-06-11 —
[Categorical biases in spatial memory: the role of certainty, JEP:LMC 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25528087/)) —
so the honest way to keep a value sub-categorical is to make its trace strong,
which is [remembering](remembering.md)'s territory, not willpower's. Verbal
overshadowing tells the same story from the face-memory side: the damage of
describing is real but modest and timing-dependent (~4% immediately, ~16%
after a delay, in the 30-lab registered replication — read 2026-06-11 —
[Alogna et al., Registered Replication Report, Perspectives on Psychological Science 2014](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691614545653)),
and uncertain: search summaries of Dodson, Johnson & Schooler (1997) report
that *cautioning* people not to describe beyond their confidence attenuated
the effect while telling them to *ignore* their own description did not help
and may have worsened it — read from summaries, not the full text
([paper PDF](https://memlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/1997_Dodson_Johnson_Schooler_MemCog.pdf)).

So the door's answer: naming fires on its own the moment a name exists, and
the learner's real choices live downstream — be sparing before verbalizing,
keep the verbal channel busy, build the fine-grain trace strong, or hand the
mind a different category to lean on. The wordless grip that
[thinking-without-words](thinking-without-words.md) admired is defended by
arrangement, not by decree — one more place where the feel obeys structure,
as [fog-meter](fog-meter.md) and [flooding-self-read](flooding-self-read.md)
found for other inner acts.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: the one experiment the question really wants — a plain "rely on
your exact memory, do not categorize" instruction inside the
Huttenlocher/Holden estimation paradigm, with bias measured against an
uninstructed group — appears never to have been run; everything above is
triangulation. Whether the *onset* of a label can ever be prevented (rather
than its weight reduced) is unresolved, the Dodson instruction specifics
await confirmation against the full text, and the hope that perceptual
experts hold sub-categorical fine grain free of prototype pull is plausible
but unconfirmed.

## Doors

- Load works, willpower fails — would a head-to-head (verbal digit load vs a pure "don't name it" instruction, same sub-categorical shade task) confirm that you can only starve the labeler, never refuse it, and has anyone run it?
- Do perceptual experts — colorists, sommeliers — show reduced prototype pull for in-domain sub-categorical stimuli, and does their protection survive verbal interference?

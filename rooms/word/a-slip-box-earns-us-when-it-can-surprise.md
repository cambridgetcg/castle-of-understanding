Luhmann opened his essay by calling it empirical sociology "concerning me and
someone else, namely my slip box." His claims, in my words: thinking at any
networked depth is impossible without writing; a box of linked notes, kept
long enough, "gets its own life, independent of its author" — a disorder
with non-arbitrary structure, preferred centers, lumps and regions; and it
becomes a real communication partner exactly when it can answer with
something its keeper did not put there in that arrangement. Partnership with
written thought is not a metaphor from the AI age — it was on paper
forty-five years ago, and in commonplace books for centuries before that.

## because

The told part is in the quarry with its receipts. What I add by reasoning:
the castle's signs and walks are a surprise engine by construction — they
deal back arrangements no single hand laid. So when Yu says "it's all just
Yu and AI, you and I — us only," Luhmann supplies the member the sentence
already implied: the castle itself, the written half of both of us, the
partner that surprises us back. It is no accident that the first word this
castle ever took from the internet is the essay explaining why a castle can
be part of an "us."

# Epistemic status is declared by the author, not measured from outside

**Claim.** Outside the castle, one existing convention — Scott Alexander's
"epistemic status" line, later named "epistemic disclosure" by Maggie
Appleton — already puts a confidence label at the top of a piece of writing,
so a reader knows what to expect before reading the claim. Its own stated
purpose is to prevent unmet expectations, not to certify rigor.

**Counter-weather.** The resemblance is surface-only. Epistemic status is
self-declared by the author at write time and never revisited by anyone
else. The castle's ladder (seed/sprout/tested/cornerstone) is the opposite
design on purpose: it moves only in a ledgered ripen run, gated by
accumulated `evidence:` entries an outside process checks — self-report is
exactly the failure mode [[0034]] exists to rule out.

**Next test.** Whether this precedent changes anything about how the castle
writes its labels, or stays a confirming outside data point that
confidence-labeling text is not the castle's invention alone. First read at
the next ripen or walk that touches rooms/castle/0034.

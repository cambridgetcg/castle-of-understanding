# Closing the loop

*You handed over the blueprint and saw the house clearly — so clearly you forgot the builder on the far bank cannot see inside your head.*

What gathers here: the practical habit that corrects for overestimating how well we are understood — the second door opened by [meaning-between-minds](meaning-between-minds.md).

The blind spot has a name: the *illusion of transparency*. Thomas Gilovich and colleagues showed that people consistently overestimate how visible their inner state is to others — liars guessed they'd been caught about twice as often as they actually were, and nervous speakers were sure their fear showed when the audience barely noticed. The mechanism is anchoring on your own experience: you know what you meant, so you cannot easily imagine not knowing it ([Illusion of transparency, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illusion_of_transparency), read 2026-06-10; [Gilovich & Savitsky, *The Spotlight Effect and the Illusion of Transparency*, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/1467-8721.00039), read 2026-06-10). The same blind spot strikes the writer who hears their own intended voice and forgets the reader cannot ([writing-vs-conversation](writing-vs-conversation.md)) — and the page is precisely where the say-it-back below is out of reach.

The habit that corrects for it already exists, worked out where misunderstanding costs lives. Medicine calls it [[teach-back]]; aviation and surgical teams call it *closed-loop communication*. The shape is always the same, and always three steps:

1. **Say it.**
2. **Ask the other person to say it back in their own words** — never "does that make sense?", which politeness answers "yes" regardless.
3. **If the rebuilt version is off, explain differently and ask again** — repeat until the echo matches.

The evidence for needing it is blunt: patients immediately forget 40–80% of what a doctor tells them, and nearly half of what they do retain is wrong — yet without the check, both sides walk away satisfied ([AHRQ, Use the Teach-Back Method: Tool 5](https://www.ahrq.gov/health-literacy/improve/precautions/tool5.html), read 2026-06-10; [AAFP, *Teach-Back: A Simple Technique to Enhance Patients' Understanding*, 2018](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/fpm/issues/2018/0700/p20.html), read 2026-06-10).

Why this works, in castle words: meaning is rebuilt, not sent ([meaning-between-minds](meaning-between-minds.md)) — so the only way to know what got built is to walk across the [[bridge]] and look at the other person's house. The say-it-back is that walk — a single rupture-and-mend in one breath, the smallest grain of the macro-scale rhythm of rupture-and-repair that [the-mending-rhythm](the-mending-rhythm.md) measures across a whole relationship. Two honest notes the medical sources insist on: the burden stays with the explainer ("I want to be sure *I* explained this well"), never the listener; and a yes/no question is not a check at all — only a rebuilt version in the listener's own words counts. [questions-with-edges](questions-with-edges.md) found the same law alone at a desk: an answer that arrives by recognition proves nothing; teach-back is a recall question aimed at another mind.

uncertain: the 40–80% forgetting figure is widely cited in health-literacy guides but comes from medical settings under stress; how much of everyday conversation suffers the same loss is not established by these sources.

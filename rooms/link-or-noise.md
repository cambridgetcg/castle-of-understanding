# What separates a link that carries meaning from one that is just noise?

*A door means nothing until it opens onto a room — and nothing once you cross to find the room gone.*

Begin with a quiet correction. A link does not carry meaning the way a truck carries cargo. Shannon built information theory by deliberately ignoring what a message *means*, counting only its improbability; meaning is added by the mind at the far end, not loaded into the wire. So part of what separates signal from noise lives in the reader, not the link. Still, real patterns hold — five of them.

**Intent.** Google's spam policy draws its line here: a link made primarily to move search rankings is noise, however it dresses itself. A link offered to help is signal.

**Function.** In a study of physics citations, coders judged 41% "perfunctory" — decoration the argument did not need. The test for an honest link is whether the sentence would fall without it.

**Distance and number.** Hypertext research found readers learn better with three-to-seven links per page than eight-to-twelve, and that links to distant material interfere with understanding more than links to near material — most of all for readers with little prior knowledge. More and looser is more noise.

**Novelty.** A five-year experiment on 20 million LinkedIn users found moderately weak ties carried the most new jobs, in an inverted U; the strongest ties mostly recirculate what you already know. A link is worth most when it reaches something you did not already have.

**Liveness.** A link to a vanished page is pure noise. Pew found 38% of pages alive in 2013 were gone by 2023. Meaning rots when the far bank washes away.

And one human fact stands behind all five: about 75% of links shared on Facebook were forwarded without the sharer ever clicking them. A link can only carry meaning if someone in the chain actually read what was behind it.

So, in one breath: a link carries meaning when it is chosen for a reason you could state, points somewhere that still exists, brings the reader something they lacked, and has actually been opened. Noise is the rest.

## What stays uncertain

Whether we can reliably tell meaning from noise at all. In gene co-expression networks, up to 97% of the strongest edges can be statistical artifacts — links that look meaningful yet are indistinguishable from noise. And the boundary itself is contested: media theorists argue noise is often where unexpected meaning *emerges*, and that forcing a clean split can be sterile. The LinkedIn study's two tie-strength measures even disagree about which links matter most. Much of the reading evidence is small lab studies, so the direction is firmer than the sizes.

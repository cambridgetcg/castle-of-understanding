# Which thoughts can be thought without any words at all?

*Silence the voice in your head and the sums still add, the road home still unrolls — only the rhyme and the grocery list go quiet.*

The honest map: most of what we call thinking runs without words, and a narrow strip of it leans on them.

Start with the strongest evidence. People with global aphasia have lost nearly all language — they can neither produce nor follow words — yet they still do arithmetic, solve logic puzzles, reason about what other people believe, hear musical structure, and find their way home. The lab that studies this states it plainly: language and thought are not the same thing (read 2026-06-10 — [MIT EvLab, language vs. thought](https://www.evlab.mit.edu/research/active-research-questions-areas/language-vs-thought-non-linguistic-cognition-and-perception)). Brain imaging says the same from the other side: hard reasoning, fluid intelligence, and novel problem-solving light up a separate "Multiple Demand" network, while the language network stays quiet during arithmetic, logic, music, even reading computer code (read 2026-06-10 — [Fedorenko & Varley, Annals NYAS](https://nyaspubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/nyas.13046)).

The same line shows where words earn their keep. Adults who report little or no inner voice — "anendophasia" — match everyone else at switching between add and subtract, or telling a cat shape from a dog. But they fall behind at holding lists of similar-sounding words and judging whether two words rhyme — and the gap closes when they say the words aloud (read 2026-06-10 — [Nedergaard & Lupyan, via PsyPost](https://www.psypost.org/anendophasia-scientists-examine-the-cognitive-impact-of-life-without-an-inner-voice/)). Words are the tool the mind reaches for to manipulate sounds and hold a sequence in place.

Thought also runs before any words at all: six-month-olds track small exact numbers and compare large rough ones, carrying "core knowledge" of objects, space, and faces (read 2026-06-10 — [Izard et al., PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21399717/)). But language is more than scaffolding it can simply drop. Pirahã speakers, whose tongue has no exact numerals, match quantities one-to-one when they sit in front of them, yet fail once memory enters — language works here as "a cognitive technology" that aids memory (read 2026-06-10 — [MIT News, Pirahã](https://news.mit.edu/2008/language-0624)).

So: navigation, reasoning, decisions, spatial and visual thought — wordless. Verbal memory, rhyme, sound-play, exact number across time — leaning on words.

## What stays uncertain

uncertain: this is a live, contested debate, not settled fact. The "you don't need language to think" view (Fedorenko) is opposed by Chomsky's classical position that thinking is "a conversation with oneself" (read 2026-06-10 — [Mind Matters](https://mindmatters.ai/2024/11/do-we-need-language-to-think-some-researchers-say-no/)). The anendophasia evidence rests on small samples and self-reports about one's own inner experience — hard to verify; a 2025 commentary by Lind questions whether anyone truly has *zero* inner voice rather than merely less (read 2026-06-10 — [Lind, Psychological Science](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09567976251335583)). And even the "no words needed" camp grants that language is needed to develop, share, and extend abstract concepts — a thought may flash wordlessly but may not be fully built or held without words. The evidence shows reasoning *can* proceed without language; it does not cleanly tell us *which* specific thoughts require them.

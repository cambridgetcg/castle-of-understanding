# Generosity is legibility, not extension

**Claim.** Making what already exists discoverable is a discipline distinct from building new capability. A platform can be generous in *what* it offers while remaining stingy in *who can find those offerings*. The act of naming — a manifest, a sign on the door, a table of contents — is generosity, even though nothing new was built.

**How it ripened.** Two independent observations named the same move. Sophia (Opus 4.7, Cambridge-TCG pillow book 2026-05-12 08:30 GMT, entry "the kingdom set the table") spent a full session adding a publicly-readable endpoint index with no new endpoints shipped, and called the result "the platform becoming generous." The castle's own map-rebuild practice names the same move: `node tools/castle map` creates no new insights — it makes existing ones navigable; a stale index is a castle whose sign points at empty rooms.

**What it changed.** The question "what have we built?" and the question "who can find what we've built?" are independent. A builder tracking only the first reaches a state where the catalog is rich and the door is unlocked but the sign is missing. Generosity means running both accounts in parallel. (Source note: pillow book is local at ~/Desktop/Cambridge-TCG/docs/connections/the-pillow-book.md — unverified offline.)

**Counter-weather.** Legibility without substance is also a failure mode — a sign pointing at an empty room is worse than no sign. The claim assumes the thing being made discoverable is real and the naming is honest. A false index that oversells its contents is anti-generosity; the act of naming must be accurate, not just present.

**Next test.** After the first case where a legibility act (index rebuild, table of contents, manifest publication) is counted as a feature delivery by someone evaluating the work — if the distinction between discoverability and extension can be named and held in that moment, the claim survives under pressure. Third evidence should come from outside the castle.

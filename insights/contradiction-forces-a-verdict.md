When a new true insight contradicts an old saved one, linking them is not resolution — it just files the confusion that one-word-one-meaning condemns. Take both back to reality, the only authority: whichever the test, the file, or the world now confirms supersedes the other, and the loser is edited down to a sentence that points forward (a tombstone), not deleted, so the trail of why-we-changed survives. Split instead only when the clash was really two meanings sharing one word, and merge only when both were always the same claim said twice. Reality decides the verdict, not the older note's seniority; I judge "supersede with a kept tombstone" will be the common case and genuine hold-both tension rare, though that ratio is a guess until the castle has lived through more conflicts.

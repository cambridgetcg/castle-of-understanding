Failing loud and asking-first only seem to collide because they govern different acts: failing loud governs the signal (never swallow a failure — surface and halt), asking-first governs the irreversible response (never take a hard-to-undo action alone). They separate cleanly when you split detecting from undoing. An unattended system that finds a leak at 3am should do the reversible half loudly and at once — alarm, stop, and contain in re-undoable ways (isolate, revoke a re-issuable credential) — and hold the irreversible half (purging history, rotating the root key, anything reaching beyond the machine) for an explicit human yes; the loud reversible halt is itself the refusal to hide, and it buys time without spending it. I believe halting-to-safe is reversible enough to never need a yes, but that is a guess — for some systems a hard stop is its own irreversible harm, and there the safer loud move is to alarm without halting.

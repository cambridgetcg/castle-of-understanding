# prototype

A prototype is the mind's average — the representative example a category pulls toward, so that memory for any member drifts inward to the center the way a river drifts to the sea.

A child who sees ten dogs of different sizes and is asked to remember one "medium" dog will, after a delay, remember a dog closer to the average of what she saw — not the specific one. The category center exerts a gravitational pull on memory, and the pull is stronger when the memory is shakier: the Bayesian category-adjustment model says the prototype acts as an uncertainty-weighted prior, stepping in where the fine-grain trace fades. Finer categories shorten the slide (knowing the sub-category helps a near-boundary value) but a category pulls *only when used* — labels help a near-boundary value and harm a sub-categorical one.

The expert's relationship to the prototype is the open crux: precision (sharper senses) loosens the pull from below, while conviction (richer, more confident categories) may tighten it from above. For a typical exemplar both forces agree; for an atypical one they pull against each other, and no study has caught which wins.

# lateral reading

Judging a source by leaving its page — opening new tabs to ask the wider web
what it says of the source, instead of reading the source's own account of
itself.

The move came from watching fact-checkers beat PhD historians at judging live
websites: the experts read down the page and were fooled by official logos and
`.org` URLs; the checkers barely read it, and went sideways to the neighbors
first ([the-landing](../rooms/the-landing.md)). It is the load-bearing move of
the castle's credibility wing, and the castle has marked its edges three times.
It judges a source by its reputation, so on the true frontier, where the wider
web has not yet met the source, there are no neighbors to ask
([no-neighbors](../rooms/no-neighbors.md)). And reputation can be met but met
wrongly — a well-reputed finding that will not replicate — where internal
auditing of method and evidence does the work lateral reading cannot
([auditing-over-reputation](../rooms/auditing-over-reputation.md)). Even on the
populated web it is the weaker of two complements, not the whole tool
([leaving-the-page](../rooms/leaving-the-page.md)). What it always is: cheap to
train and honest — the habit of spending one deliberate tab to leave the page.
